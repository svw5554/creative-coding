Japan

About Japan

Japan is a fascinating land of contrasts, a heavy mix of tradition and modernity that often bewilders but never bores. Nowhere in the world blends the old and new quite like Japan. The speed of new technological developments here is matched only by the longevity of its ancient customs and traditions. The country is a pioneer in the fields of design, technology, and fashion. You can set your watch by the trains, eat meals that look like works of contemporary and relieve yourself in the most technologically advanced toilets on the planet.

Cities To Visit

Kyoto

The ancient cultural mecca of Japan. Kyoto is traditional culture and pastimes reign in what was once the nation’s political capital, and now its rightful cultural capital. It is home to a number of temples, shrines, and some of the country’s most incredible natural landscapes.

Tokyo

Japan's capital and the world's most populous metropolis. TTokyo is a crazy, frenetic, and astounding city. Here you can visit the imperial palace, the morning fish market, see the beautiful cherry blossoms, party in Tokyo’s trendy nightlife district, sing karaoke, and eat lots of amazing food.

Osaka

The second most prominent city of Japan. Dotonbori and Shinsekai have a bustling, exotic vibe rarely matched elsewhere, while the streets around Osaka Station present a more refined, polished aesthetic, allowing travelers to experience the many faces of Japan all in one city.

Must Eats

Ramen

It refers to a noodle soup made with four basic components – the broth, tare (seasoning), wheat noodles, and toppings. There are four main types – shoyu, miso, shio, and tonkotsu.

Kaiseki Ryori

A traditional multi-course meal in Japan consisting of over ten small artfully prepared Japanese dishes. Kaiseki typically consists of appetizers, a soup course (suimono), seasonal courses, cooked dishes, a rice course (shokuji), and dessert and tea.

Sukiyaki

A popular Japanese hot pot dish which is often cooked and served at the table, similar to Shabu Shabu except that the food is cooked in a sweet and salty soy sauce-based broth and full of bold flavors straight from the pot.

Hitsumabushi

Unagi is served in a bowl over rice with yakumi (condiments) and dashi as a side dish. The first stage involves eating the eel over rice as is. Then mix the yakumi into your bowl in the second stage. When you’re down to your last few bites, you pour the dashi (sometimes tea) into your bowl and finish the rest.

Sushi

Sushi refers to a family of artfully presented dishes made with vinegared sushi rice and a variety of ingredients, mostly raw fish and other types of seafood.

Takoyaki

Ball-shaped snack made from wheat flour batter filled with minced or diced octopus, tempura scraps, pickled ginger, and green onions. It’s usually topped with a sweet and savory takoyaki sauce, Japanese mayo, green laver, and bonito fish flakes.

Yakitori

Refers specifically to skewered and charcoal-grilled chicken, though it’s often used to describe skewered meat in general, both poultry and non-poultry.

Miso Soup

Soup made with dashi, miso paste, and any number of optional ingredients like vegetables, tofu, abura-age (deep-fried tofu pouches), fish, and seafood. It’s a Japanese food staple that’s served with many set meals in Japan.

Natto

a traditional Japanese dish consisting of fermented soybeans and characterized by a slimy, sticky and stringy texture. In Japan, natto is typically topped with soy sauce, mustard, chives or other seasonings and served with cooked rice.

Oden

A type of nabemono (Japanese one-pot dish) made with any number of ingredients like chikuwa, konnyaku, ganmodoki, daikon, and boiled eggs stewed in a soy-flavored dashi broth.
